# Why Legacy ERP Needs a Modern Communication Layer

Last Tuesday’s post made the point that most ERP systems are still 10+ years old because they still run the business. That is the reality. These systems remain embedded because replacement is hard, risky and often commercially unclear.

But that creates the next problem.

If the ERP core is staying in place, the layers around it cannot stay stuck in the same decade.

That is where many businesses now have a serious gap. The ERP may still process the transaction, but the communication around that transaction is often slow, fragmented, manual or badly outdated. That is what customers feel. That is what operations teams work around. And that is where a lot of hidden friction lives.

## ERP may run the process, but it does not guarantee the experience

This is one of the biggest misunderstandings in enterprise technology.  People assume that if the ERP is doing the heavy lifting, the business is covered. Orders are processed. Invoices are raised. statements are generated. Despatch notes exist. Compliance documents are produced. So, everything must be fine.  Right?

Usually, it is not because the transaction engine and the communication layer are not the same thing.

An ERP system might know what happened. That does not mean it communicates it well. It does not mean the right document reaches the right person in the right format at the right time. It does not mean output is clean, branded, channel-appropriate or easy to change. It does not mean workflows between ERP data and customer communication are efficient.

That distinction matters more than ever. Deloitte said in March 2026 that ERP modernisation is increasingly about building broader business capability without weakening control. In other words, the market is shifting beyond the old idea that ERP is just a back-office system of record. It is now part of a wider operating model that must support speed, automation and better decision-making.

The trouble is that many legacy ERP environments were never designed to handle modern communication expectations.

## The communication layer is usually where the cracks show first

This is what businesses often live with for years without fully naming it.

Customer documents come out in old formats. Output changes are slow and expensive. Teams rely on manual workarounds. Different business units create their own templates. Communications are inconsistent across print, PDF, email and digital channels. Compliance changes take too long to implement. Data is trapped in the ERP, but the customer-facing experience around it feels disconnected.

The ERP still works. The communication does not, which is the real issue.

PwC has repeatedly argued that legacy systems and siloed processes reduce agility and make it harder for organisations to respond quickly and integrate effectively. That lands directly here. The weakness is not always the transaction engine itself. It is the brittle layer around it that turns ERP data into customer-facing output and operational communication.

So, when a business says, “Our ERP is fine,” what they often mean is, “The core transaction engine still works.”  That is not the same as saying the communication model is fit for purpose, certainly in this modern world.  A world that the ERP was not born in.

## Why this matters commercially

This is not just a document design problem. It is not a nice-to-have branding exercise. It is not a side project for operations.  It is commercial, because customer communication shapes how the business is experienced.

Invoices, order confirmations, delivery notes, policy documents, statements, renewal notices, remittance advice, claims correspondence, regulated communications, internal workflow documents. These are not admin leftovers. They are part of the operating model. They affect accuracy, responsiveness, customer confidence, compliance risk and cost-to-serve.

When the communication layer is weak, businesses pay for it in ways that often stay hidden:
delays, rework, service calls, exception handling, fragmented ownership, slow change cycles and poor customer experience.

And the irony is obvious. Companies will spend millions debating ERP replacement while tolerating obvious communication failures around the core for years.

That is backwards.

## The ERP is staying longer, not shorter

This is why the communication issue matters now.

If businesses were all replacing ERP immediately, you could argue that it is smarter to wait. But that is not what the market is telling us.

SAP still provides mainstream maintenance for SAP Business Suite 7 core applications until the end of 2027, with optional extended maintenance through the end of 2030. Oracle announced in March 2026 that Premier Support for Oracle E-Business Suite 12.2 is now extended through at least 2037. Those are not short holding patterns. Those are long operational time horizons.

That means many businesses will continue to run older ERP cores for years yet. So, the question becomes brutally practical:

If the ERP is staying, how long are you willing to leave the communication layer underpowered?

That is the real decision.

## A modern communication layer changes the economics

This is where the conversation gets interesting.  A modern communication layer allows a business to improve the customer-facing and operational experience around ERP without having to rip out the ERP itself.

That means you can modernise output without replacing the core transaction engine. You can improve document design, channel delivery, workflow control and change responsiveness without turning the whole programme into a multi-year ERP transformation.

That is a very different economic proposition.

Instead of saying, “We must replace ERP to improve communication,” the business can say, “We can improve communication now, while the ERP remains in place.”

That changes the timing. It changes the risk. It changes the spend profile. And frankly, it often produces value faster.

McKinsey has long pointed out how difficult ERP transformations are, noting that around three-quarters fail to stay on schedule or on budget and two-thirds generate negative ROI. That does not mean ERP modernisation should not happen. It means businesses are right to be cautious about betting everything on core replacement when there are more targeted ways to improve operational outcomes.

## Why this is the right lens for Objectif Lune

This is exactly why Objectif Lune belongs in the conversation.  Not as a cosmetic add-on. Not as a print tool. Not as “document composition” in the narrow old sense but as a practical communication and output automation layer around ERP.

That is the point.

Where a legacy ERP system holds the transaction logic, Objectif Lune can help shape, control and modernise how that information is turned into communication. Customer documents. Operational output. Workflow-triggered communications. Multi-channel delivery. Process steps that need to move beyond static ERP-generated output.

That is valuable because the communication layer is often where ERP environments show their age fastest.

And it is also valuable because it is a realistic route to progress. Businesses may not replace the core this year. They may not replace it for five years. Some may not replace it for ten but they still need better output, better communication and better process around the core now.

## This is not about making legacy look pretty

It is worth being clear here.

A modern communication layer is not about putting better fonts on an old process and pretending the job is done.  It is about fixing a real operational weakness.

It is about making customer communication more accurate, more timely, more flexible and easier to govern. It is about reducing the dependency on manual workarounds. It is about making output changes easier to deliver. It is about improving how ERP data gets translated into something the customer, supplier, citizen, patient or partner experiences.

That is not cosmetic. That is operational value and in a lot of businesses, it is one of the most overlooked areas of automation.

## The real lesson

If legacy ERP is staying in place for longer than people expected, then it needs a better layer around it. Not eventually. Now.

Because the core may still run the transaction, but the communication layer is often where the customer experience, operational friction and change bottlenecks really sit.

That is why legacy ERP needs a modern communication layer.  Not because the ERP is useless because the business around it has moved on.
