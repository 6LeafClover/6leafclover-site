# Why Most Automation Projects Don’t Deliver ROI

Automation should make a business better.  That is the point.  Not to create a clever demo.  Not to buy another platform.  Not to tell the board that “we are on a transformation journey.”

The real reason to automate is simple.  To improve the numbers that matter. Cost. Speed. Accuracy. Compliance. Margin. Customer experience.

And yet, most automation projects do not fully deliver that return.

That is not because the technology is weak. Quite the opposite. The market for workflow and process automation keeps growing fast, which tells you businesses still believe in the opportunity. Grand View Research estimates the intelligent process automation market was worth $14.55 billion in 2024 and projects it to reach $44.74 billion by 2030. Fortune Business Insights says the workflow automation market was worth $25.1 billion in 2025 and projects it to reach $65.26 billion by 2034.

So, the demand is real.  The investment is real.  The problem is that ROI is still often missing.

McKinsey has been saying this for years. Its research found that fewer than 20% of organisations had scaled automation across multiple parts of the business, even though many believed a quarter of their work could be automated. In an earlier survey, McKinsey also found that only a slight majority of organisations had met their automation targets. On the wider transformation point, McKinsey found that less than 30% of transformations succeed, and only 16% of digital transformations both improved performance and sustained the change.

That should make every leadership team stop and think.  Because the issue is usually not automation itself.  The issue is how organisations approach it.

## The real reason ROI goes missing

Most automation projects fail to deliver ROI for six very boring reasons.

They are boring because they are not about shiny software.  They are about management discipline.  And that is exactly why they matter.

## 1. They automate a broken process

This is the classic mistake.  A messy process gets automated before it gets fixed.

The result is that the business does the wrong thing faster.

Deloitte’s automation research has repeatedly found that process fragmentation is the number one barrier to automation. In its 2022 survey, Deloitte said the top barriers were process fragmentation, lack of a clear vision, lack of IT readiness and resistance to change. It also noted that immature and fragmented processes had been the top barrier for four straight surveys.

This is the first trap.  If the process is full of exceptions, workarounds, duplicate steps and local habits, automation will not create ROI. It will simply hard code the chaos.

## 2. They start with tools, not outcomes

A lot of projects start with the software.

“We bought RPA.”  
“We bought workflow.”  
“We bought low code.”  
“We bought OCR.”

Fine. But why?

If the answer is not tied to a clear business outcome, the ROI will be fuzzy from day one.

Deloitte found that 22% of organisations did not have a clear and accepted vision for intelligent automation, and 41% did not have an enterprise-wide automation strategy. That is a huge warning sign. If the business cannot clearly explain what value it is trying to create, the project usually turns into activity without impact.

Good automation starts the other way round.

What are we trying to improve?  
What metric moves?  
What cost comes out?  
What revenue risk drops?  
What customer problem disappears?

Start there. Understand the current process and what you want to achieve first.  Then pick the tools.

## 3. They never build a proper baseline

This is where many ROI stories fall apart.

People say the automation is “going well,” but they cannot prove it.  The project team is busy.  The dashboard looks nice.  The board still has no idea whether value has been created.

Deloitte found that over half of respondents had not calculated cost reduction and 70% had not calculated the increase in revenue expected from intelligent automation. Even among implementing and scaling organisations, more than half had not calculated the proportion of the workforce impacted.

That, my friends, is brutal.

If you do not baseline effort, error rates, turnaround time, rework, headcount demand, customer friction and compliance risk before the project starts, then ROI becomes guesswork after go-live.

Unfortunately, boards do not fund guesswork for long.

## 4. They ignore the people who do the work

Automation is often sold as a technology project.  IT IS NOT.  It is an operating model change.

The people doing the work need to understand what is changing, why it is changing, and what good looks like afterwards. If they do not, adoption stalls, exceptions grow and shadow processes appear.

Again, Deloitte’s findings are clear. Resistance to change remains one of the top barriers. It also found that workforce support was much stronger in organisations already implementing and scaling automation than in those still piloting it, which suggests that communication, training and positive experience matter a great deal.

This should not be a surprise.  If the team thinks automation is being done to them rather than for the business and with them, ROI leaks out all over the floor.

## 5. They run too many pilots and too little scale

This one is a silent killer.

A business launches five pilots in five departments.  All of them sound promising.

None of them change the enterprise.  McKinsey’s survey found that adoption of automation technologies was climbing, but fewer than 20% had scaled across multiple parts of the business.

That matters because local wins are not the same as enterprise ROI.  One small automation in accounts payable might save a bit of time.  However, real value usually comes from redesigning the end-to-end flow across teams, systems and handoffs.

That is a different level of leadership.

## 6. They do not deal with legacy reality

This matters more than many vendors admit.

Most businesses do not run in a perfect greenfield world. They run with old ERP, old workflows, old documents, old exceptions and old habits. That is real life.

Deloitte’s 2025 Process Mining Survey says many companies still rely on legacy systems, making integration complex and requiring hybrid approaches. It also found that 41% cited management support as a barrier to adoption, up from 26% in 2021. In other words, old estates are still hard, and leadership alignment is still weak.

This is why automation programmes drift.  The software demo assumes a clean process because the demo and the technology was selected before anyone had a full understanding of the process and the issues.  The business runs on twenty years of patches, manual fixes and tribal knowledge.

Unless somebody leads the hard work of joining those two worlds together, the expected ROI stays in the PowerPoint.

## So, what does deliver ROI?

The answer is not mysterious.  The organisations that get value tend to do the same things well.

They choose a process that matters.  
They tie it to a measurable business outcome.  
They simplify the process before automating it.  
They baseline the economics properly.  
They assign clear ownership.  
They involve operations early.  
They deal with integration honestly.  
They track value after go-live, not just technical completion.

Deloitte’s 2025 Process Mining Survey points to four value levers: strong centres of excellence, executive support, integration with AI and automation technologies, and continuous process optimisation. That last point matters a lot. Automation is not a one-off event. It is a management discipline.

That is the real why.  Automation exists to make the business work better.  Not just look more modern.

## The leadership question

If you are sponsoring an automation programme, there are a few hard questions worth asking now:

- What business result are we trying to improve?
- What is the baseline today?
- Who owns the number?
- Have we understood and simplified the process first?
- Are we automating a task, or fixing an end-to-end workflow?
- Have we budgeted for change, training and adoption?
- How will we prove value 6 and 12 months after go-live?

If those questions are fuzzy, the ROI probably will be too.

## Final thought

Most automation projects do not fail because the software fails.  They fail because businesses treat automation as a technology purchase instead of a business change programme.

That is the mistake.

Automation can absolutely deliver ROI. The evidence says the opportunity is huge. But the return does not come from buying the tool. It comes from understanding first, redesigning work, leading change, measuring properly and staying focused on outcomes.

That is less glamorous.  It is also where the money is.
