## Why Automation Projects Fail (Even When the Tech Works)

Here’s a scenario you’ve probably seen before. Or maybe you’re living it today. The technology is live. The dashboards look good. The vendor says it’s a success because you signed off against the scope and the test plan.

But the business process you actually wanted to change? Still slow. Still manual. Everyone is still frustrated. So what on earth went wrong?

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Most automation projects don’t fail because of technology. They fail because of everything around it. And the data is pretty clear on this.

McKinsey tells us that less than 30% of transformation programmes succeed. For digital transformation specifically, that drops to around 16% delivering sustained performance improvement. A further 7% saw some improvement but could not sustain it.

At the same time, global IT spend continues to climb — forecast to exceed **$5.6 trillion**, with over **$1.2 trillion in software alone**.

So businesses are investing heavily, but struggling to turn that investment into real, lasting outcomes. Actually delivering on the promise and the idea that started the project.

**That’s not a technology problem. That’s an execution problem.**

---

## Here’s where things break

### 1. Bad process, automated faster

If you automate a broken process, you just get bad outcomes faster. The accepted theory is that all you need to do is replicate what you are doing manually today into a tech stack and hey presto, Bob’s your uncle.

But how much work was done to look at the process and redesign it before applying technology? Where was the simplification?

In reality, all you have done is speed up a broken process.

---

### 2. No link to real outcomes

A very simple question to ask:

**“What changed on the P&L?”**

I do wonder if anyone can answer it. If not, then you don’t have transformation or business change.

You just have activity that you spent time and money on.

---

### 3. Too many stakeholders, no decision

I often talk about the Elephant Sandwich — a project that is simply too big to eat.

In the sandwich, everyone is involved but no one is in charge. The project grows because it is easier to add new ideas than make decisions. The new ideas dilute the business case. So decisions drag. Compromises creep in.

The end result pleases no one because the project may never get signed off — and if it does, you end up back at points one and two.

---

### 4. No plan after go-live

So you got your transformation project signed off. There are committees and working groups.

At some point, it becomes a technical exercise rather than business transformation.

This is the killer.

Go-live is treated as the finish line because it’s seen as a technical milestone.

**It isn’t. It’s the start.**

---

## The reality on the ground

Even in areas where automation should be easy to achieve — like invoice processing — many organisations are still heavily manual.

Research shows:

- Over half of finance teams spend more than 10 hours a week processing invoices  
- Around 60% still manually key invoice data into ERP systems  
- Nearly three quarters say they are only partially automated  

This is one of the most mature, well-understood automation use cases.

And it’s still not fully working.

That tells you everything.

---

## What actually works

The projects that succeed all look the same:

- Start with process, not tech  
- Tie everything to measurable outcomes (no Elephant Sandwiches)  
- Keep decision-making tight  
- Assign real ownership  
- Plan for optimisation from day one  

---

## The bottom line

The gap isn’t investment.

**The gap is execution.**

Automation only matters if it changes something real:
- Cost  
- Speed  
- Risk  
- Customer experience  

If it doesn’t…

**It’s just expensive plumbing.**
