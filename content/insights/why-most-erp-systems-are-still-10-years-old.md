# Why Most ERP Systems Are Still 10+ Years Old

A couple of weeks back, we said legacy ERP is still one of the biggest untapped automation markets in the enterprise. This week let’s deal with the obvious question. If these systems are so old, why are they still everywhere?

Because most of them still run the business.

That is the bit people love to ignore. They talk about old ERP as if it is just bad technology, poor leadership or corporate laziness. Usually, it is none of those things. Usually, it is a rational business decision.

ERP systems are not a side project. They sit in the middle of finance, operations, manufacturing, supply chain, customer fulfilment and compliance. They hold the rules, the transactions and the process logic that businesses rely on every day. McKinsey recently made exactly this point, arguing that leaders often focus only on ERP technical debt and forget the “equity” built into those systems over years of real operational use.

## The simple answer

Most ERP systems are still 10+ years old because replacing them is hard, expensive, disruptive and often commercially unclear.

That may not be a fashionable answer, but it is the real one.

Yet the market keeps modernising. Deloitte said in March 2026 that ERP modernisation investment is rising, with 43% of organisations in its 2025 study investing in ERP, up from 35% in 2024. But that does not mean everyone is ripping out the old core. In parallel, SAP cited IDC research saying more than two thirds of large enterprises globally still use on-premise ERP systems. So, the reality is not “old ERP is gone.” The reality is “modernisation is happening, but old ERP is still deeply embedded.”

That matters because people often confuse market noise with installed-base reality. Vendors talk about the future. Customers live with the past and present at the same time.

## ERP is old because it is important

Nobody leaves a non-critical system in place for fifteen years just for the fun of it.

ERP lasts because it usually does something essential. It processes orders. It closes the books. It manages stock. It controls purchasing. It supports production. It handles revenue recognition, invoicing and fulfilment. When a system sits at the centre of those activities, “let’s replace it” sounds simple on a slide and much less simple in real life.

That is why some of the biggest enterprise application estates are still supported for the long haul. SAP Business Suite 7 mainstream maintenance runs to the end of 2027, with optional extended maintenance through 2030. Oracle says Oracle E-Business Suite Premier Support runs through at least 2037, explicitly positioning that support model around customer choice and no forced migrations. Those are not the support timelines of a market where everyone has already moved on.

So, when people say, “Why are companies still on old ERP?”, the answer is often: because the vendor still supports it, the business still depends on it, and the case for ripping it out is weaker than people pretend.

## Replacement is not a technology decision

This is where a lot of ERP conversations go wrong. They get framed as technology decisions when they are really business risk decisions.

A full ERP replacement is not just buying new software. It means reworking processes, integrations, data structures, reports, outputs, controls, training, change management, partner delivery, programme governance and business ownership. It also means living through the disruption while still trying to run the company.

That is why ERP transformations have such a mixed track record. McKinsey has been making this point for years: ERP programmes are never easy and the implementation model, operating discipline and delivery approach matter enormously.

And that is before you even get into the messier truth: many businesses do not have the appetite, budget or organisational energy for a core replacement unless there is a burning platform.

So, they do what most sensible businesses do. They tolerate the old core and try to improve the world around it.

## Old does not always mean broken

There is also a snobbery problem in enterprise tech.  People hear “10+ year old ERP” and assume the business is asleep at the wheel. Sometimes that is true. Often it is not.

A lot of older ERP estates are stable, customised around real operating needs and deeply understood by the people who use them. Some are ugly. Some are clunky. Some are expensive to support but many still do the job they were bought to do.

The real problem is usually not that the ERP system exists. The real problem is that the layers around it have not kept up. The communication layer. The document layer. The workflow layer. The integration layer. The customer experience layer.

That is where friction creeps in.

PwC has made the broader point that legacy ERP systems and siloed processes hold businesses back by limiting agility and integration. Deloitte has also highlighted that legacy infrastructure often makes newer AI and automation approaches harder to connect and scale. In other words, the issue is often not just the core transaction engine. It is the surrounding environment that has become brittle and disconnected.

## The economics are usually the killer

This is the bit that gets conveniently ignored by people selling transformation.  A business will put up with a lot if the current system still works.  If the old ERP is ugly but reliable, management will ask a brutal but fair question: what do we get for the money, risk and disruption of replacing it?

That question is getting sharper, not softer. The ERP market itself is still growing, and Mordor Intelligence estimated cloud deployments held just over 55% of ERP market share in 2025, while hybrid is forecast to grow strongly through 2031. That tells you something important. The destination may be newer platforms but the journey is still mixed. Full clean-slate replacement is not the only path customers are taking.

And when a company looks at the cost of a major ERP programme, then looks at a list of other priorities, the result is predictable. The old system survives another planning cycle.

Then another.

Then another.

That is how 10 years becomes 15, and 15 becomes 20.

## There is a difference between modernising and replacing

A business does not need to replace ERP to modernise the customer-facing and operational experience around ERP.

That is the real opportunity.

The documents can improve. The communications can improve. The workflows can improve. The integrations can improve. The digital experience can improve. The handoffs can improve. The visibility can improve. The process around the core can become faster, cleaner and more commercial without detonating the whole platform.

That is why old ERP persists. Not because nobody sees the problem but because many businesses have quietly decided that selective modernisation around the core is smarter than wholesale replacement of the core.

Frankly, in a lot of cases they are right.

## The real lesson

If most ERP systems are still 10+ years old, the lesson is not that enterprises are backward.

The lesson is that the enterprise software market still misunderstands how businesses make risk decisions.

Companies keep old ERP for rational reasons:  stability, sunk investment, process dependency, limited appetite for disruption, vendor support lifecycles and unclear replacement ROI. The installed base remains huge, modernisation is rising, but the core is still often staying put.

So, the real question is not, “Why haven’t they replaced it yet?”

The better question is, “How do we create more value around the system they already trust?”

That is where the serious automation opportunity sits.

If the ERP core is staying in place for longer than people expected, then it needs a better communication layer around it. A modern business cannot afford to leave customer documents, operational output and communication workflows stuck in the same decade as the underlying ERP.
