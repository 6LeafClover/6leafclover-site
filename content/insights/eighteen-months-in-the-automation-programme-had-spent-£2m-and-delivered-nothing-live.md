A case study exploring one of the most common reasons ambitious automation programmes stall: not the technology, but the governance around it.

### The situation

Bellweather Insurance had committed serious investment to an automation programme intended to overhaul claims intake and processing. Eighteen months and roughly £2M in, not a single process had reached production. The technology stack was reasonable. The architecture, on paper, was sound.

### What was actually wrong

Spending time inside the programme revealed the real issue quickly. There was no single accountable owner for architecture decisions — instead, a committee that met monthly and rarely resolved anything definitively. Workstreams that depended on each other's outputs had no visibility into each other's timelines, so each was quietly waiting on the others without anyone realising it. Blockers were being managed politely, in private conversations, rather than surfaced where they could actually be resolved.

### The reset

Rather than restarting the programme, we ran a structured two-week assessment: mapping every workstream against what had genuinely been built versus what had simply been assumed or planned. This produced an honest, sometimes uncomfortable, picture of true progress.

From there, we installed a simple governance structure:

- A single accountable owner for architecture and design decisions, with the authority to actually make them
- A weekly delivery forum where every workstream lead was required to name specific blockers, not general progress updates
- A visible dependency map, so teams could see what they were waiting on and why, rather than discovering it weeks later

### The result

The first automated process went live within six weeks of the reset — the programme's first production deployment in eighteen months. Four more followed within the same quarter, using momentum and confidence the team hadn't had in over a year.

### The lesson

A stalled automation programme is rarely a sign that the technology is wrong. Far more often, it's a sign that nobody has the authority, the visibility, or the forum to make the decisions that would unstick it. Fix the governance and the technology usually starts moving on its own.
