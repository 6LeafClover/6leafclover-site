\### The situation



Thornbury Print Solutions ( a real reseller whose name has been changed) had sold and supported Objectif Lune for several years, and knew the platform well technically. Commercially, however, the sales conversation had settled into a single, narrow shape: positioning Objectif Lune as a modern replacement for whatever legacy output tool a prospect was currently using. It worked, in a limited way, but it left significant capability of the platform entirely unsold.



\### What was being missed



Working through Thornbury's existing customer base and the platform's broader capability, three genuinely differentiated use cases stood out — each one the platform already supported well, and each one Thornbury had never packaged as a distinct, sellable offer:



\- \*\*Dynamic customer communications\*\*, tailoring document content and format to individual recipient context rather than producing static output

\- \*\*Automated compliance documentation\*\*, generating and archiving records with the audit trail regulated customers increasingly need

\- \*\*Unified multi-channel output\*\*, producing print, email and portal-ready documents from a single workflow rather than three separate processes



\### Turning capability into proposition



Each use case was built into a named, one-page proposition with its own pricing structure, target customer profile and specific talking points — deliberately separate from the general "Objectif Lune platform" conversation, so sales conversations could open with a concrete problem rather than a broad product pitch.



\### The result



Within six months, average deal size on new Objectif Lune engagements rose by 35%. The shift wasn't a change in the underlying technology or pricing — it was a change in the opening question, from "would you like to replace your current system?" to "here's a specific problem this solves, does it sound familiar?"



\### The lesson



Holding a strong licence and technical capability is only half the commercial picture. The other half is packaging that capability into a small number of clear, named propositions a customer can recognise their own problem in. Without that step, even excellent technology tends to get sold as a commodity.
