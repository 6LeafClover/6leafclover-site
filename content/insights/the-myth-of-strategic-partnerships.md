# The Myth of Strategic Partnerships

Let’s stay with the same theme from the last post about Partnerships.

We said that most partner programmes do not produce revenue. One of the biggest reasons is this: too many companies confuse a relationship with a strategy. That is where the idea of the “strategic partnership” starts to fall apart.

They talk about strategic partnerships as if saying the words makes them true. A logo goes on a slide. A few senior people have a meeting. A press release gets written. Someone says there is “strong alignment.” Then six months later, nothing has changed. No pipeline. No new revenue. No real joint motion. Just another relationship being politely admired from a distance. No new pipeline. No joint selling rhythm. No meaningful revenue impact.

They look at a partner and see what could happen.  
Big customer base. Good brand. Strong market presence. Technical overlap. Smart people.

All of that can be true and still produce nothing.

That is not a strategic partnership. That is a well-presented idea.

## The Why

A real strategic partnership should create an advantage that neither business could create as effectively on its own.

That could mean better market access. It could mean stronger solution value. It could mean faster deal progression or better customer outcomes. But whatever form it takes, it has to produce something measurable.

That is the key point.

A partnership is not strategic because the companies involved are large. It is not strategic because there is executive sponsorship. It is not strategic because both sides like the story. It only becomes strategic when it changes commercial outcomes. That is where many partner programmes get themselves into trouble. They become fascinated by the label and forget to test the result.

## Where the Myth Starts

In a lot of organisations, “strategic partnership” really means “a partner we would like to matter.” That is a very different thing.

There may be genuine potential in the relationship. The partner may have a strong brand, a big customer base or a very relevant offer. On paper, it can look like a perfect fit. But potential is not the same as performance. Plenty of partnerships look brilliant in theory and do absolutely nothing in practice.

The problem is usually not bad intent. It is a lack of commercial precision.

Businesses often spend a lot of time talking about alignment at the top of the relationship. They discuss vision, market opportunity and shared ambition. All of that is fine, but it is not enough. If the practical mechanics are missing, the partnership never gets off the ground.

Sooner or later, somebody must answer the hard questions.

- What are we actually taking to market together?
- Who owns the number?
- Which opportunities are in scope?
- How will the field engage?
- Why would a seller prioritise this over something else?

If those questions are still vague, the partnership is still vague.

## Why So Many “Strategic” Partnerships Go Nowhere

This is where the myth becomes expensive.

A lot of businesses carry around a long list of so-called strategic partners but only a tiny number of them create any real value. The rest sit there as respected names in a deck or a partner portal, adding more perceived importance than actual contribution.

That creates two problems.

First, leadership ends up with a false sense of progress. The ecosystem looks stronger than it really is because the names sound impressive. Second, the business spreads its time and energy too thinly across relationships that have not earned that level of focus.

The best strategic partnerships are often much more focused than people expect. They are usually built around a clear use case, a defined customer problem and a repeatable commercial motion. They are not broad because somebody declared them strategic. They become strategic because they repeatedly produce results.

That is a big difference. The relationship does not come first. The working model does.

## What a Real Strategic Partnership Looks Like

A real strategic partnership is not built on vague positivity. It is built on execution.

Both sides know why the relationship exists. Both sides understand where they can win together. There is clarity around the offer, the target market and the route into accounts. The people in the field know what to do with it. Progress can be measured. Revenue can be seen.

That is what makes it real.

It is also why the strongest partnerships often start smaller than expected. They do not try to boil the ocean. They begin with one solid commercial motion and prove that it works. They earn the right to expand because the first thing delivered value.

This is the bit many companies miss. They want the status of a strategic partnership before they have done the work to make one function.

But strategic is not a title. It is a result.

## The Test

So here is the simplest way to cut through the noise.

Ask what the partnership has actually changed.

Has it created pipeline that would not otherwise exist? Has it improved access to customers? Has it increased win rates, improved proposition value or opened new routes to revenue? Has it changed the numbers in a way that matters?

If the answer is no, then the honest answer is also no. It may still be a useful relationship. It may still have promise. But it is not yet strategic.

That honesty is useful, because once you stop flattering partnerships, you can start managing them properly. You can focus on the ones that matter. You can put real structure around them. You can move from partner theatre to partner performance.

And that is where partner-led growth starts to become real.
