### The starting point

Halborn Software (names have been changed..) had spent five years signing partners. Every trade show brought a handful of new logos. Every logo went into the directory. On paper, the programme looked substantial — over 400 partners spanning three continents.

In practice, when we mapped revenue against the partner base, a different picture emerged. Just 7% of partners were generating meaningful pipeline. The rest were dormant: signed once, onboarded loosely, never followed up.

### Why this happens

It rarely happens through negligence. It happens because signing partners feels like progress, while managing a smaller number well feels, wrongly, like doing less. Nobody wants to be the person who says no to a new partner. So the estate grows, and the effort needed to serve it thins out to match.

### What changed

We ran a structured segmentation exercise across the full partner base — revenue contribution, engagement history, strategic fit and growth potential — and used it to make three honest decisions:

- Which partners to invest in further
- Which needed a different kind of relationship, such as a lighter-touch or marketplace model
- Which relationships had run their course

Halborn moved from 420 partners to 160. It felt, briefly, like a step backwards. It wasn't. With clearer focus, account teams could run proper business planning with the partners that mattered, rather than sending the same generic newsletter to everyone.

### The result

Within two quarters, channel-sourced pipeline was up 210%. Not because there were more partners in the programme, but because the partners still in it were finally being properly supported.

### The lesson

A long partner list looks like reach. A short, well-managed one produces revenue. If your channel feels busy but the pipeline isn't moving, the first question worth asking isn't "who else can we sign?" — it's "who's actually in the room, and are we treating them like it matters?"
