# Automation Projects Don’t Fail — They Drift

A few weeks ago, we talked about why most automation projects do not deliver ROI.

The short version was simple. Automation should improve the numbers that matter. Cost. Speed. Accuracy. Compliance. Margin. Customer experience.

But too often, it does not.

Not because the technology is weak. Not because the demo was fake. Not because the idea was wrong.

More often, automation projects fail for a much quieter reason.

They drift.

They drift away from the original business problem. They drift away from the people who need to use them. They drift away from the commercial outcome that justified the investment in the first place.

And by the time someone notices, the project has not obviously failed. It has just become another thing the business has bought, implemented, half-adopted and quietly worked around.

That is the dangerous bit.

Failure is easy to spot. Drift is not.

## Automation does not usually fail in one big moment

Most automation projects do not collapse dramatically.

There is rarely one big meeting where everyone admits the project has gone wrong. Nobody stands up and says, “Right, that’s it. We have officially wasted a lot of money.”

It is usually much softer than that.

The scope changes. The business owner gets pulled onto something else. The delivery team starts focusing on technical completion rather than business impact. Users get trained, but not really embedded. Reporting gets thinner. Benefits tracking gets pushed into the future. Everyone stays polite.

Then six months later, the automation exists, but the value is unclear.

The workflow is live, but people are still using spreadsheets.

The dashboard is there, but nobody trusts the data.

The process has been “digitised”, but the same old exceptions are still being handled manually.

That is drift.

## The market is growing, but that does not mean value is guaranteed

This is the uncomfortable part.

The automation market is booming. Grand View Research estimates the global intelligent process automation market was worth **$14.55 billion in 2024** and expects it to reach **$44.74 billion by 2030**, growing at a CAGR of **22.6%**. So, businesses clearly still believe in automation. They are still buying. They are still investing. They are still trying to remove friction, reduce errors and improve efficiency.

But market growth does not prove project success.

It proves demand.

And demand is not the same as delivered value.

In fact, the more companies invest in automation, AI and digital transformation, the more important it becomes to ask a basic question: are these projects actually changing how the business works, or are they just adding more technology into an already messy operating model?

That question matters because investment can hide drift for a long time.

When a project has executive attention, a budget, a steering committee and a vendor behind it, it can look healthy even when the business value is slowly leaking away.

## Drift starts when the project loses its “why”

Every good automation project should start with a clear business reason.

Not “we want to automate.”

That is not a reason.

The reason should be something like:

We need to reduce manual rework in order processing.

We need to cut the time it takes to onboard a customer.

We need to reduce compliance risk in document handling.

We need to improve margin by removing low-value manual effort.

We need to increase capacity without adding headcount.

That is the real “why”.

The problem is that once the project starts, the original business reason often gets diluted. The conversation moves from business outcome to technical delivery. Requirements take over. Workshops multiply. Configuration decisions become the centre of gravity. Everyone gets busy.

Before long, the question changes from:

“Will this improve the business?”

to:

“Can we get this live?”

Those are not the same question.

Going live is not success. It is just the point where the business starts finding out whether the work was worth doing.

## Poorly scoped automation creates expensive confusion

Gartner made a similar point recently in the context of AI in infrastructure and operations. It said that stalled or failed initiatives are often driven by projects that are **overly ambitious or poorly scoped**, and that AI which does not fit into the organisation’s operations cannot deliver ROI.

That lands directly in process automation as well.

If the automation is not properly scoped around the way the business actually works, it will struggle.

If it automates a process nobody has properly understood, it will struggle.

If it depends on data that is incomplete, inconsistent or badly governed, it will struggle.

If the people using it are not part of the change, it will struggle.

This is where businesses sometimes confuse automation with magic. They take a broken process, put technology over the top, and expect a better outcome.

But automation does not remove poor process thinking.

It exposes it.

Sometimes it even accelerates it.

A bad manual process is painful. A bad automated process is painful at scale.

## Drift happens in the gap between design and reality

On paper, most automation projects make sense.

The process map looks clean. The workflow looks logical. The business case looks believable. The vendor presentation looks convincing. Everyone nods.

Then the project meets reality.

Reality is where the exceptions live.

Reality is where teams have created workarounds for things nobody documented.

Reality is where customer data is incomplete, approvals are inconsistent, handoffs are unclear and people do things slightly differently in every region, team or department.

That is where automation either becomes useful or starts to drift.

The best projects adjust to reality without losing the original outcome. The weaker ones either ignore reality or let reality slowly drag the project away from its purpose.

That is why governance matters.

Not governance as in heavy meetings, traffic-light reports and endless programme admin.

Real governance.

The kind that keeps asking:

Is this still solving the problem we started with?

Are the right people using it?

Is the process actually improving?

Are the benefits showing up?

What needs to change?

Without that discipline, drift is almost guaranteed.

## Adoption is where the truth comes out

You can tell a lot about an automation project by what people do after go-live.

If they use it naturally, trust it and stop using the old workaround, you might have something valuable.

If they keep the spreadsheet “just in case”, you have a problem.

If they still email each other outside the workflow, you have a problem.

If managers still ask for manual reports because they do not trust the system, you have a problem.

If the process technically exists but nobody owns the outcome, you have a problem.

The issue is not always resistance. Sometimes the users are right.

Sometimes the automation is clunky. Sometimes it does not reflect the real process. Sometimes it makes one team’s life easier by making another team’s life harder. Sometimes it removes visible work but creates hidden work somewhere else.

That is why adoption cannot be treated as a training task at the end.

Adoption is not a launch activity.

It is a design principle.

## AI is making the drift problem more visible

AI is now being pulled into automation conversations everywhere.

That is not surprising. Citigroup recently raised its view of the global AI market to more than **$4.2 trillion by 2030**, with enterprise AI representing a major part of that forecast.

But the same rule applies.

Bigger market. Bigger investment. Bigger expectations.

Still no guarantee of value.

Deloitte has talked about the AI ROI paradox: investment keeps rising, but returns remain unclear for many organisations. Its 2025 survey found that businesses continue to invest because AI is seen as a strategic necessity, even where ROI is still difficult to prove.

That is exactly how drift gets funded.

Everyone believes the direction is right, so the investment continues. But unless the project is tied to a specific business outcome, the value can remain vague for far too long.

Automation and AI do not fail just because the technology is immature.

They fail because the business does not stay close enough to the outcome.

## The warning signs are usually there

Drift is not invisible if you know what to look for.

The warning signs are usually quite clear.

The project is still active, but nobody can explain the benefit in plain English.

The original sponsor has disappeared.

The business case is never mentioned anymore.

Success is being measured by delivery milestones, not operational improvement.

Users are working around the system.

The process owner is unclear.

The vendor is driving the agenda more than the business.

The reporting focuses on activity rather than value.

None of these things means the project is doomed. But they are warning lights.

And the earlier you spot them, the easier it is to pull the project back on course.

## The fix is not more technology

When automation drifts, the answer is usually not to buy another platform.

That is often the worst answer.

The answer is to get back to the business problem.

What are we trying to improve?

Who owns it?

How does it work today?

Where does the friction really sit?

What does good look like?

How will we know if it is working?

Who needs to change their behaviour?

What happens after go-live?

These are basic questions, but they are often the ones that get skipped because everyone is too busy building.

That is a mistake.

Automation is not just a build activity. It is a business change activity.

And business change needs ownership.

## The real lesson

Automation projects do not usually fail because nobody cared.

They fail because the project gradually moves away from the outcome.

A little bit at a time.

One scope change. One weak handover. One missed adoption issue. One unclear owner. One benefit that never gets measured.

That is drift.

And it is more common than outright failure.

The lesson is simple.

If you want automation to deliver, you have to keep dragging it back to the business result. Again and again.

Not the workflow.

Not the software.

Not the demo.

The result.

Because automation only matters if it changes the way the business performs.
