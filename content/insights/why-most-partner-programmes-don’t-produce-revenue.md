Let’s be honest.

Most partner programmes look great on a slide and do very little in the real world. There are logos everywhere, there’s an impressive lineup of “Strategic Partnerships,” and the slides are full of fancy tiering models.

But you sit in the team meeting, and the same question — the elephant in the room — keeps coming back:  “How much revenue did partners actually generate last quarter?”  And then things go quiet.

What Does the Market Say?

This is not a niche problem.

GTIA’s State of the Channel report says that around 70% of global technology spend now flows through partners and the channel. Forrester points the same way: partner ecosystems are growing in size and importance, and 67% of surveyed B2B leaders expect indirect revenue to grow above or significantly above last year’s level.

In simple terms: if your partner model isn’t working, you’re missing a big chunk of the market.


Here’s what’s really going wrong

	1. Who Owns The Number
In many organisations, Partners sit in marketing but revenue sits in sales.  No one is truly accountable or want to take responsibility to own the number.

So activity goes up but revenue doesn’t.  Everyone gets frustrated and the Elephant comes back in the room.

	2. Too Many Partners, not Enough Focus
You run a killer campaign and sign lots of new partners. The list of partner logos grows to two slides; the business and Marketing are delighted.

Yet very few organisations manage these partners properly. You created the tiers and the lists, but didn’t put in place the methodologies to manage them — and voilà, back to square one.
You don’t need 100 partners.

You need 10 that actually sell — and you need to manage them properly.

	3. No Co-Sell Discipline
“Let’s work together” sounds great. You create a partnership, you have a couple of great dinners, maybe even a few balloons and streamers at a launch event.
But a few months later, there’s no shared pipeline, no joint account planning, and no deal ownership.

So deals in the pipeline drift… or die.  Without discipline, the leads and opportunities will dry up. Partnerships are great — but only if you build structure and discipline.


	4. Incentives Don’t Align
If your sales team gets paid more to sell direct, what do you think they’ll do? And what will the business quietly encourage?

Will your partners trust you not to take opportunities direct? Many organisations claim to be partner‑driven, then take a customer direct faster than you can say, “But don’t we have a contract?”

Partner sales teams don’t need a new iPad or an Amazon voucher.  They want your trust and your help.  Partners don’t fail. Incentives do.


	5. No Results or Consistency
If you don’t have structure or cadence — and you don’t have honest performance tracking — then you’re relying on hope.


What Good Looks Like

A proper partner model is simple:

	• Clear revenue ownership
	• Focused partner set
	• Structured co-sell
	• Aligned incentives
	• Regular performance reviews

That’s it.


The Bottom Line
The market and the analysts tell us the opportunity is huge — which makes poor execution even more expensive. Partners are not a strategy. They are a revenue engine and like any engine, they need structure, discipline, and accountability.

Otherwise, you’re just collecting logos… and elephants
